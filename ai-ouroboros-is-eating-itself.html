The Ouroboros Problem: When AI Starts Eating Its Own Tail

As AI-generated words, images, and music flood the web, newer models begin to learn from the output of older ones. The result is sameness, stagnation, and, in the worst case, model collapse.

By William Furtado October 30, 2025 6 min read

“Don’t worry, the Ouroboros of AI is slowly eating itself,” my tweet said. If the image is vivid, it’s because the risk is real. As editors, creators, and media platforms run more content through AI filters, newer models start to learn from the older models' output. The loop tightens. The result is not superintelligence, but rather sameness and slow degradation of the signal.

This issue stems from a few trends. First, synthetic content is cheap if not outright “free,” and the stock of clean, licensed human data is limited. Datasets - a key to training a model, are almost entirely made by other GPT-like AI. We can go even one layer deeper when AI is training AI, and despite the learning being relational and exponential, it is, most importantly, entirely artificial. As humans not only rely on the data generated by AI but also filter the humanness of data through AI, the degradation loop begins, and each iteration becomes warped, misunderstood, and inherently corrupted.

This can be seen when one draws a picture and then asks a GPT to make an exact copy, then to copy that copy over and over. Faces melt into intangible forms, pictures begin to show an unnatural yellow “piss filter,” and music degrades into noise.

People fret and complain AI will replace us -- but only if we allow it. AI needs us; we are an endless source of creative data (yes, you are now essentially a matrix-style battery powering Generative Artificial Intelligence with your brain, and I am sorry to be the one to tell you), and the only thing it doesn't want to do is replace us. If we lose our humanity, AI loses its usefulness, as these things are intrinsically tied.
