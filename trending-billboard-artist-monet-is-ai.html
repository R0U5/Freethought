AI Generated Singer gets signed.

Meet Xania Monet, a virtual R&B/Gospel act assembled with AI by Suno, with the lyrics being written (as far as we know) by Mississippi poet Telisha “Nikki” Jones.

By William Furtado October 30, 2025 3 min read

Monet just became, by Billboard’s count, the first AI act to debut on a Billboard radio airplay list, entering Adult R&B Airplay at No. 30 with “How Was I Supposed to Know?”. I won't sit here and pontificate on the potentially unholy alliance that, somehow, has formed between good old god fearing human beings and a collection of god-less numbers and letters streaming as pseudo-consciousness, I'll just join the club.

I prompted ChatGPT 5 to look up Monet online, and then I linked it to a few of my articles. I then prompted it with "here are some blog posts I wrote. Analyze the style, tone, and words I use. I want you to write an article about the AI singer in the same tone and messaging I would use". This is what you get when we filter human creativity through an AI's artificial lens. You be the judge:


Monet, the AI singer, and the cost of synthetic stardom

I hit play on a voice that felt church-bred and studio-polished. It also wasn’t a person.If you’re trying to decide whether that’s progress or parody, the industry already voted with a pen. Hallwood Media signed Monet to a multimillion-dollar deal after a label bidding war; multiple outlets peg the figure at around $3 million. Now the part that matters. If the voice, melody, and performance are machine-generated, what, exactly, did anyone buy? Copyright law still treats AI outputs as uncopyrightable without meaningful human authorship. In Monet’s case, experts say only the human-written lyrics are clearly protectable. That’s not a nitpick; it’s the whole business. Is there a world where this stays useful? Maybe. If labels are transparent about provenance. If credits reflect real labor. If “AI-assisted” means human-led writing, performance, and production with tools in support, not in place, of musicians. If the data feeding these systems is licensed and traceable. None of that is solved today. See the active lawsuits around training data and the Copyright Office stance. Monet isn’t the villain. She’s a mirror. We’re the ones deciding whether convenience beats craft. If you care about the signal—write, sing, hire, license, credit. Keep people in the loop. Because once the loop eats us, there’s not much left to learn from.

I think we can clearly see while the model has not trouble with using my words, it does not capture my tone, or understands my intention. The same thing can same can be said for AI generated music. It will be there, in elevators, on phones, in waiting rooms -- anywhere it is cheaper to generate sounds in a pleasent manor without having to pay an artist.

Music, like genius, is messy. It cannot be accrately be replicated in the same manor, it is only a facismile.
