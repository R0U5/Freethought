Trending Artist Monet is not Human.

Meet Xania Monet, a virtual R&B/Gospel act assembled with Suno.

By William Furtado November 11, 2025 1 min read

Meet Xania Monet, a virtual R&B/Gospel act assembled with Suno, lyrics by Mississippi poet Telisha “Nikki” Jones. Monet (who I will refer to as the “AI” moving forward to ensure there is no misunderstanding exactly what we are talking about) just became, by Billboard’s count, the first AI act to debut on a Billboard radio airplay list, entering Adult R&B Airplay at No. 30 with “How Was I Supposed to Know?” This isn't the first time the AI has seen some success, with a previous Gospel Song getting noticed on the Host Gospel Songs charts curated by various streaming services, such as Spotify.

If you are starting to be overwhelmed by the sense of irony, I would say you are not the only one. I suspect that most of these song plays are coming from Spotify playlists where the listener is not aware that the “soulful” gospel sounds landing upon their ears are not being sung by a person with conviction and faith, but rather they have been painstakingly constructed by a godless AI. If they did know, would it matter to them at all? Perhaps it would just be considered art filtered through another instrument, albeit one that has a much stronger influence on the artist and its creation compared to something like, say, the Tuba?

So, are we looking at the future of Music or another AI-based clickbait headline? Copyright law still treats AI outputs as uncopyrightable without meaningful human authorship. In Monet’s case, experts say only the human-written lyrics are clearly protectable. That being the case, when an artist uses protectable material but provides it via a non-protected platform, where does it land as far as copyright is concerned? When Banksy left a mural on the wall of a private business, the private business owner was allowed to see that portion of his wall. It did not matter that Banksy did not intend for the owner to do so; it only mattered that the medium was somebody else’s.

It will be interesting to see how this all plays out, given the rise of AI and its use to game the Spotify ecosystem. It will be even more interesting to see if Ms.Telisha can really grow or even maintain her numbers. The exposure here works both ways and will likely land her a few hate comments referring to her creation as “AI Slop”. All in all, I don’t think this is really something that we should consider harmful as much as it is deceptive. People may feel differently about the artist praising Jesus through the Gospel if they knew it was being done by a piece of software. Time will tell.
