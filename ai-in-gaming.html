AI in Gaming: Depth or Slop?

AI can deepen quests and NPCs—or flood stores with slop. What happens next depends on taste, restraint, and craft.

By William Furtado November 4, 2025 4 min read

AI in gaming could do two things: enrich play with more varied quests and better NPC interactions, or flood stores with AI-generated asset-store slop. Only time will tell.

Like it or not, AI is already part of daily life and will likely shape your next favorite game.

If not the model work, then the writing. Not the writing? Then the NPC barks.

Splurged on human writers? Congrats: AAA budget, indie ethics.

The real problem starts when the small developers and the players who support them (already contending with a cacophony of Unity assets that long predates the label of "AI slop"), one could even argue that this is almost entirely responsible for Unity’s negative reputation in some circles.

I imagine that when these low-effort models start being generated on a large scale, they will start flooding the asset store.

This means that while indie game devs will see a notable drop in the prices of these assets, they will likely also see a qualitative drop as the AI Ouroboros starts slowly eating itself by training new models using data from previously AI-generated ones.

Now, the other side of the same coin would be cheap access to assets, improved output via text or model generation, or even systems tied to ChatGPT.

A great example of this is the Skyrim mods that turn all NPCs into (albeit very easily confused) chatbots, such as Herika and Mantella.

What this shows me is that the developers with talent, with passion -- the ones with "true grit" will go beyond just surviving; they will flourish as the tools and choices that were previously only available to the largest developers become available to all who want them.

Given the presence of LLMs in everyday life, from the chatbot at the local marijuana dispensary to using ChatGPT to congratulate co-workers, the future is unavoidable; gaming AI is inevitable; and we can only hope that it will add to our favorite games, and not replace the heart and soul of those who passionately develop them.
